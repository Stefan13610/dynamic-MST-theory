Dynamic Mass-Space-Time Theory

Basic Assumptions of the Theory

This theory posits that mass generates both space and time. The underlying concept suggests that massive bodies not only curve space-time but dynamically produce it. Thus, the observable properties of the universe, such as the speed of light, could result from the total mass and the scale of the universe itself.

One of the intriguing implications of this theory is that the speed of light, c, may not be a fundamental constant but instead a value emergent from the mass and structure of the entire universe.

Derivation of the Speed of Light without Dark Matter

Without including dark matter, the speed of light can be approximated using the following expression:

c ≈ sqrt(G * M / R)

where:

  • G is the gravitational constant, approximately 6.674 × 10-11 m3 kg-1 s-2,
  • M is the estimated baryonic mass of the universe, approximately 1.5 × 1053 kg,
  • R is the approximate radius of the observable universe, approximately 4.4 × 1026 meters.

Substituting these values:

c ≈ sqrt((6.674 × 10-11 m3 kg-1 s-2) × (1.5 × 1053 kg) / (4.4 × 1026 m))

Resulting in an approximate value of:

c ≈ 1.55 × 108 m/s

Derivation of the Speed of Light with Dark Matter Considered

When including the total mass (baryonic and dark matter) with a factor adjustment to account for the influence of dark matter (5.9253), the formula becomes:

c ≈ sqrt(G * (5.9253 * M) / R)

Using the same values for G and R as before, but adjusting M:

c ≈ sqrt((6.674 × 10-11 m3 kg-1 s-2) × (5.9253 × 1.5 × 1053 kg) / (4.4 × 1026 m))

This results in an approximate value very close to the observed speed of light:

c ≈ 3 × 108 m/s
